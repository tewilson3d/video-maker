The 180-Degree Rule (Axis of Action):

Rule: Imagine an invisible line connecting two characters in a scene or running along the direction of a character's movement. All camera setups for that scene should stay on one side of that line.
Purpose: Maintains consistent spatial relationships for the viewer, preventing disorientation (Character A is always on the left, Character B on the right, or a character is always moving left-to-right).

When to Break the 180-Degree Rule:
Deliberate Disorientation: To make the audience feel uneasy, confused, or mirror a character's mental state.
Crossing the Line Clearly: Introduce a new shot (like a shot straight down the axis, or a clear establishing shot from the new side) or have a character physically move across the line within the frame before cutting to the new angle.
Action Across the Line: If the action itself crosses the line (e.g., a car chase turning around), the camera can follow, re-establishing the axis on the new side.
Jump Cuts (Sometimes): In stylistic sequences like montages or rapid-fire edits, crossing the line can contribute to a sense of chaotic energy, though it's often combined with other "rule breaks."

Show, Don't Just Tell (How to Show Actions):
Focus on the Action Itself: Don't rely solely on dialogue or narration. Show the character doing the thing.
Break Down Complex Actions: If an action is multi-step, use a series of shots (like inserts or close-ups) to build it piece by piece (e.g., unlocking a door: hand on knob, key going in, turning the lock, door opening).
Show the Effect of the Action: Instead of just showing someone punch a wall, show the hole in the wall and the character's bruised hand.

 Extreme Wide Shot (EWS): Shows scale, environment, isolation.
 Medium Shot (MS): Waist up; good for dialogue, showing interaction, character performance.
Close-Up (CU): Head/shoulders; emphasizes emotion, shows detail, creates intimacy or intensity.
Extreme Close-Up (ECU): Isolates a specific feature (eyes, mouth, an object); creates high intensity, highlights critical details, can feel invasive.
Over-the-Shoulder (OTS): Shows interaction between two characters, placing the viewer in the perspective of one looking at the other.
Point of View (POV): Shows exactly what a character sees; creates immersion and empathy.

Composition (What's in the Frame & Where):
Rule of Thirds: Avoid centering subjects unless for specific impact (symmetry, power, stillness). Place points of interest along the lines or intersections of an imaginary 3x3 grid.
Leading Lines: Use lines within the frame (roads, fences, walls) to draw the viewer's eye towards the subject or a key part of the frame.
Framing: Use natural elements within the scene (doorways, windows, tree branches) to frame your subject, adding depth and context.
Negative Space: Empty space around a subject can convey loneliness, smallness, or draw attention to the subject by contrast.
Headroom & Looking Room: Leave appropriate space above a subject's head and more space in the direction they are looking or moving to balance the frame and guide the eye.

Continuity of Action:
Matching on Action: Cutting from one shot to another while a character is in the middle of an action. This makes the cut feel invisible and smooth (e.g., character sitting down - cut as they lower themselves into the chair).
Matching Eyelines: Ensure characters appear to be looking at each other correctly when cutting between their shots in a conversation.
Props & Set Dressing: Ensure objects, costumes, and the environment remain consistent between takes and shots within a scene (unless a change is deliberate).

Camera Movement (Purposeful Motion):
Meaningful Movement: Don't move the camera just because you can. Movement should serve the story – revealing information (pan/tilt), following action (tracking/dolly), showing scale (crane), creating unease (handheld), or building tension (slow push-in).
Smooth vs. Handheld: Smooth movement (dolly, crane, steadicam) can feel polished, grand, or calm. Handheld can feel immediate, chaotic, raw, or personal. Choose based on the desired effect.

Utilize Depth of Field:
Shallow DoF: Subject is sharp, background/foreground is blurred. Isolates the subject, creates intimacy, guides the eye, can mask distracting backgrounds.
Deep DoF: Everything is sharp. Shows relationship between subject and environment, allows the viewer to look around the frame, good for complex blocking or information-heavy scenes.

Lighting & Color:
Mood and Tone: Lighting and color are crucial for establishing atmosphere (e.g., high contrast for drama, soft diffused light for romance, cold blue tones for isolation, warm golden for nostalgia).
Highlighting Subjects: Use light to draw attention to key characters or objects.
Symbolism: Colors can have symbolic meaning that reinforces the narrative.

Sound Design:
Enhances Visuals: Sound (dialogue, music, sound effects) is not separate from visual storytelling; it's integral. Use sound to build tension, convey emotion, provide information (off-screen sound), and add realism or stylization.
